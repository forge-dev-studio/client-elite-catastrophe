Tree Fell on Your House: Who Pays and What to Do First

The sound is hard to forget. A crack, a rush of limbs, then a weight settling onto the house that was never supposed to be there. Every storm season puts trees on roofs across Rome and Northwest Georgia, and every time it happens the homeowner standing in the yard has the same two questions. Is everyone safe? And who is going to pay for this?

This guide answers both, in the right order. It is the same walkthrough we give homeowners on the phone at 2 a.m. Nothing here is legal advice or a promise about your specific policy. It is how these situations usually play out, learned from working storm jobs alongside insurance adjusters.

The first hour: stay out from under it

Before any thought of insurance, get the people part right.

  • Get everyone out and count heads. If the tree came through the roof, or you can hear the structure creaking and shifting, leave the house and stay out until someone qualified says it is safe.
  • Treat the tree as unstable. A tree resting on a house is under load in ways you cannot see from the ground. Limbs are spring-loaded, and the trunk can roll or slide without warning. Do not walk under it, and do not let kids or curious neighbors near it for pictures.
  • Check gas and power. If you smell gas, leave immediately and call the utility from a safe distance. If power lines are down or tangled in the canopy, assume they are live and call the power company. No tree is worth touching a line over.
  • Take photos from a safe distance. Wide shots of the whole scene first: the tree, the roof, the yard. Then closer shots of visible damage. These pictures are the start of your claim, so take more than you think you need.

Once people are safe and the scene is documented, the money questions can wait an hour. The tree is not going anywhere.

Who pays? Start with where the tree landed

Here is the rule that surprises almost everyone: in most cases, the insurance that pays is the policy covering the property that got hit, not the person who owned the tree.

If your neighbor's healthy oak fails in a storm and lands on your roof, your homeowners policy is the one that responds. It sounds backwards, but insurers treat a storm-felled living tree as an act of nature. Nobody is at fault when straight-line winds take down a green, healthy tree, so each policy covers its own side of the property line. The same logic runs the other direction: if your tree lands on your neighbor's garage, their policy handles their garage.

That means the awkward conversation over the fence usually matters less than people fear. You are not suing your neighbor, and they are not suing you. Each of you calls your own carrier and pays your own deductible.

The exception: a known, ignored hazard

Negligence changes the picture. If the tree that fell was visibly dead or dying, and its owner knew about it and did nothing, their liability coverage can come into play. The key word is knew. That is why documentation matters so much before a tree ever falls.

If a dead tree next door worries you, put your concern in writing. A dated letter or text message with photos, kept where you can find it, turns "everybody could see it was dead" into actual evidence. Some homeowners go further and have a tree professional put a written opinion on file. If that tree later fails onto your house, your insurer has something real to work with when it pursues the neighbor's carrier. Without a paper trail, proving that anyone knew anything is close to impossible.

What homeowners insurance typically covers

Policies differ, but most standard homeowners policies handle a fallen tree along these lines:

  • Damage to the structure. The roof, walls, windows, and the interior damage from rain that followed the hole are generally covered, minus your deductible. Attached structures like porches usually follow the house. Detached structures such as sheds and fences are typically covered too, often at lower limits.
  • Getting the tree off the structure. When a tree is on a covered structure, the cost of removing it from that structure is normally part of the claim.
  • Debris removal is where the limits show up. If the tree fell in the yard and hit nothing, many policies pay little or nothing to haul it away. When they do pay, it is often capped at a modest per-tree amount, commonly somewhere around $500 to $1,000+ per tree. Figures like these are national industry ballparks, not quotes, and every policy and every job is different, so read your own declarations page or ask your agent.

The practical takeaway: a tree on the house is usually a claim. A tree on the lawn is usually a cleanup bill.

The tree fell on the car instead

Different policy entirely. Damage to a vehicle from a falling tree runs through the comprehensive portion of your auto insurance, not your homeowners policy. That holds even when it was your own tree in your own driveway. If you carry liability-only auto coverage, there is usually no coverage for the car at all, no matter whose tree it was, unless the negligence exception above applies to a neighbor's known-dead tree.

The claim, step by step

Order matters here. Done in the right sequence, each step protects the next one.

  • 1. Document everything first. Photos and video before anything gets moved. Adjusters settle claims on evidence, and the scene will never be this intact again.
  • 2. Mitigate further damage. Policies require homeowners to take reasonable steps to keep the damage from getting worse. In practice, that means getting the hole covered fast. Professional roof tarping after the tree comes off keeps the next rain out of your living room, and the cost of reasonable mitigation is typically part of the claim. Keep every receipt.
  • 3. Call your insurance company. Report the claim promptly, describe the damage plainly, and ask two questions: what is my deductible, and does my policy cover removing the tree from the structure? Write down the claim number and the adjuster's name.
  • 4. Get the tree off safely. Removing a tree from a house is rigging work, not chainsaw work. Sections have to be lifted or roped off the structure so the weight never rolls through the roof a second time. This is exactly what an emergency tree service crew does all storm season, and it is worth waiting the extra hour for people who do it every week.
  • 5. Save invoices and stay reachable. The adjuster may want to see the tree, the invoices, and your photos. Keep the wood on site if asked, keep the paperwork in one folder, and the claim moves faster.
