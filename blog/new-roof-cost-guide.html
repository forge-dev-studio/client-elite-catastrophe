What a New Roof Really Costs (and What Drives the Price)

Roof replacement cost is the number homeowners dread asking about, partly because the answers online swing so wildly and partly because a roof is the least fun way imaginable to spend real money. So let us do this the useful way: an honest ballpark first, then a plain-English tour of exactly what pushes the number up or down, so that when the bids arrive you can read them like someone who knows what they are looking at.

The Honest National Ballpark

For a full asphalt shingle replacement on a typical single-family home, the national ballpark runs roughly $8,000 to $20,000+. Smaller, simpler, single-story roofs can come in under that; big, steep, cut-up roofs go over it.

Roofers talk in "squares," and it is worth learning the word because every bid will use it. A square is 100 square feet of roof surface. A modest ranch might be 18 to 22 squares; a large two-story with gables and dormers can be 35+. Installed asphalt roofing commonly runs about $400 to $700+ per square nationally once labor, materials, tear-off, and disposal are rolled in. Multiply squares by rate and you can sanity-check any bid on the back of an envelope.

Now the required honesty: these are national ballpark figures, not quotes. Every roof, every house, and every job differs, and the only number that counts is the one on a written estimate after someone has measured and walked your actual roof. Use the ranges to catch bids that are suspiciously far outside them, in either direction.

What Moves the Number

Size, pitch, and stories

Size sets the baseline, but pitch and height multiply it. A steep roof is slower and more dangerous to work, needs more safety equipment, and wastes more material at the cuts. A second or third story means more staging and more time hauling everything up and down. Same shingle, very different labor.

Tear-off layers

Everything up there has to come off before anything goes on. One old layer is standard. Two layers, which older homes sometimes carry from a previous shortcut, mean nearly double the tear-off labor and disposal weight, and that shows up in the price.

Decking surprises

Under the shingles sits the wood decking, and nobody knows its true condition until tear-off exposes it. Soft, rotted, or delaminated sheathing must be replaced before new shingles go down. Good contractors price a per-sheet rate for decking replacement in the bid so a surprise becomes arithmetic rather than an argument. Budget-wise, assume at least a few sheets on any roof that has ever leaked.

Flashing and penetrations

Chimneys, skylights, vent pipes, and wall intersections are where roofs actually leak, and they are where the careful money goes. New flashing, new boots, and proper detailing at every penetration take time and skill. A bid that is quiet about flashing is a bid planning to reuse the old, tired metal.

Material tier

Basic three-tab shingles are the entry point. Architectural shingles, the mid-tier standard on most homes now, cost more and typically last on the order of 25 to 30+ years with a thicker, dimensional look. Designer shingles and specialty materials climb from there. Underlayment quality, ridge caps, and ventilation components add their own smaller swings.

Asphalt vs Metal in Plain Terms

Metal roof cost runs meaningfully higher upfront: nationally, often in the neighborhood of $15,000 to $40,000+ for a typical home, or roughly two to three times a comparable asphalt job. What you buy for that is lifespan and durability. A quality metal roof can run 40 to 70+ years, sheds rain and debris well, and handles weather that sends shingles into the yard.

The plain-terms decision usually comes down to time horizon. Planning to be in the house for decades? Metal's lifetime math gets attractive, because one metal roof can outlast two asphalt roofs. Likely to sell within ten years? Asphalt almost always pencils better, since you will not be there to collect metal's long tail of value. Neither answer is wrong; they are different bets on how long you are staying.

Repair or Replace?

Not every ailing roof needs replacing, and a good contractor will say so. The logic runs on four questions. How old is it? An asphalt roof past 20 years is usually a replace conversation, because repairs buy little time on shingles that are near the end everywhere. How widespread is the damage? A few wind-lifted shingles on one slope is a repair, commonly in the low hundreds to $1,500+ range nationally depending on scope, with the same disclaimer as everything above. Damage scattered across multiple slopes points at replacement. Can the shingles even be matched? Discontinued colors and sun-faded fields mean visible patchwork. And is the decking sound underneath?

Storm damage changes the calculus in one important way: it is sudden and event-driven rather than gradual wear, which is exactly the distinction insurers care about. Wind and hail from one datable storm can turn what looked like a repair decision into a covered replacement, which is why post-storm documentation matters so much. That triage and documentation is the heart of storm damage restoration work.

Why the Cheapest Bid Is Usually the Most Expensive Roof

When three bids arrive and one is dramatically lower, that bid is rarely a bargain. It is usually a different, smaller job wearing the same name. The gap tends to hide in predictable places: no tear-off (shingling over the old layer), reused flashing, bargain underlayment or none at the eaves, no decking allowance so every soft sheet becomes a mid-job change order, and no line for ventilation. You get a roof that looks right from the street and starts failing at the details in year five.

Storm seasons add their own version: crews that appear from out of town after a big hail event, quote low, work fast, and are unreachable by the time problems surface. The defense is the same boring diligence that works everywhere in contracting. Get the scope in writing with the tear-off, underlayment, flashing, decking rate, and ventilation spelled out. Ask for proof of insurance and references from jobs old enough to have been through weather. Local and reachable beats cheap and gone.

When Storms Caused It

If wind, hail, or a tree is why you are reading this, the money conversation changes shape: homeowners insurance commonly covers storm-caused roof damage, minus your deductible, and the process has its own rules about documentation, adjusters, and timelines.
